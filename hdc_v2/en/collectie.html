The collection

Sculptures · Drawings · Paintings

The small show-room of the studio dwelling, currently an educational room, houses a large collection of portraits in plaster: most of them are children's portraits and a whole series of well-known Flemish people from the Interbellum. There are sculptures of priest-poet Jan Hammenecker (1878–1932), of dramaturge Anton Van De Velde (1895–1983), youth leader Ernest Van Der Hallen (1898–1948), bibliographer Joris Baers (1888–1975), politics woman Maria Baers (1883–1959), painter Hubert Wolfs (1899–1937) and not to forget Guido Gezelle (1830–1899). These portrait heads and busts were made in the beginning of the 1930s.

His paintings were also kept there as well as in the other rooms of the house, with highlights like the beautiful Sight on Dijle river (1924) and the Portrait of my ill mother (1923).

The museum building

In the museum building, a selection of 150 sculptures and 75 drawings can be admired. The sculptures include small etchings (bozzetti) and life-size models in plaster for assignments the artist produced on commission. The small sculptures in wood and stone constitute the most valuable core of the collection, because it includes many tailles directes, which are obviously quite unique pieces.

Iconographically speaking, the array of sculptures can be categorised into profane and religious work. The presence of religiously inspired sculptures is due to the buyers of Herman De Cuyper's work: the Catholic Flemish milieu from the period between the two wars. One of the artist's most treasured desires had always been to rejuvenate religious art in his homeland. In those times, church wardens were important clients of sacral art.

Religious work

As from 1937, Herman De Cuyper reached an animist phase, even so in his religious themes. A splendid example of this is the sketch in bluestone for the Madonna retable in the chapel of the Klein-Seminarie in Hoogstraten (1938) or Notre Dame pregnant, a crucial piece in marble from 1937.

Profane work: mother and child

Among the profane works, Mother-and-child portrayals are the most numerous ones: these are tailles directes, in most cases small in dimensions, with a dominant frontal view and in which he always strived for an introverted model.

Very often De Cuyper used the da prima-technique in bluestone and practised the contradiction between finished and unfinished as a constitutive element of the sculpture freed from matter: this adds an extra dimension to the co-creation of he who contemplates the art. Polished flesh regions contrast with a roughed background, thus highlighting the human face.

The intimistic representations of the important moments of life (love, mating, pregnancy, birth, motherhood, family and the world of a child) are worked out plastically by Herman De Cuyper into a metaphysics of the human face. He creates sculptures that spiritualise the human condition and elevate it to a higher level. Profane and religious merge into one and the same level.

The drawings adorning the walls are almost entirely devoted to the world of the child. These portraits were made with simple materials (pencil, charcoal, chalk and sanguine) mainly on white paper. Their craftsmanship and spiritual expression show great affinity with the graphic work of Albert Van Dyck (1902–1951), considered one of the leading exponents of animism in the world of painting.
